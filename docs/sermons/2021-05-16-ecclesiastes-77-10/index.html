Solomon has been talking to us about the school of hard knocks. That sorrow and rebuke and even death, he says, have beneficial aspects to them. This is something the world does not comprehend, but the Christian does. Fourthly, tonight. You must be able to avoid vain regrets that personally grow. Notice verse 10. Say not thou, what is the cause that the former days were better than these? But thou dost not inquire wisely concerning this. Boy, here’s another hindrance to our learning from our experiences in this life. And that is that we’re thinking always about the good old days. Or perhaps we’re concentrating of what we should have done what our life would be like if we had gone a different direction in the past instead of the path we already took. Now think about this. Suppose you were driving to Texas and you were going to go on the interstate, how do you think you would do? Do you think you could drive safely if you were always looking in the rear view mirror or worse, looking at the back window of your car? or the whole way down? You think that would be a good idea? Can you imagine driving, you know, 70 miles an hour or 75 or whatever the case may be, with your eyes glued to the back of your car instead of looking in front of you? And so Solomon is saying here is you can’t live life by constantly looking behind you. You think that’s a wise thing to do? If you crash a car, you’re going to crash your life doing that. It’s not that history is useless. History has a good purpose, and you can learn from your past experiences, but what he is concerned about is this discontentment in the present that longs for the days of yesteryear and to turn back the clock to some golden age when it’s already passed, pining for the old days which seem to appear better and brighter and less difficult, he says, is not wise. Do you recall Israel? during their wanderings in the Sinai desert actually longed for their days of slavery in Egypt. It’s almost laughable when you think about it. We had leeks and garlic and onions and dried fish. Yeah, but you were slaves. Some people never recover from the loss what they believe were the happiest days of their lives. Some Christians long for the excitement that first accompanied them when they came to know the Lord. There are churches with great histories that may look upon the days of yesteryear when they were more influential, maybe specifically more blessed of God in those days. But this isn’t wise, Solomon says. Those days are gone. You can’t relive them. You have to live in the present and look forward to the future. The pines for the past is not the way to live at this time. You must seek God’s present blessings, not whine about what might have been. I like what Paul writes in Philippians 3, 3, 13, and 14, forgetting those things which are behind, and reaching forth under those things which are before, I pressed toward the mark for the prize of the high calling of God in Christ Jesus. There’s things we have to just forget about. They’re past. Let’s focus on the present. Let’s press for the mark that God has laid out before us. Matthew Henry said this. We have to wait upon God. We have to learn to control our anger and the frustrations that we have in this life. We have to learn to avoid vain regrets about the past, live for the present, live for the future. Because that’s all we have. We can’t undo what’s done. And so this is what Solomon is telling us, I think, in this text tonight. May we keep this in mind as we go through our Christians.
